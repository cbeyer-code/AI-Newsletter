## Highlights of the Week

Richard Campbell talks brilliantly about the current AI hype, at what stage of the hype cycle we currently are, and what will remain of the hype once the market adjusts.   
He points out that **we humans have a lot of agency in the upcoming decisions that will shape the development and application of AI within our societies**. So inform yourself and participate in those conversations. For example, participate in the discussions on how our study programs should adjust in the light of AI (Studiengangsgespräch).  
<https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uWnUnMphmPM>  
  
Jaron Lanier takes a similar approach, and as Richard rejects the perspective of considering AI as its own (maybe conscious) entity, but rather encourages **viewing AI always as a collaboration of people**.  
 People produce vast amounts of research and training data, and therefore should also have share in the use of AI. He suggests using counterfactual cluster estimation to figure out jailbreak attempts by users, but also to **identify and pay relevant contributors whose training data was used to create the answer by an LLM**. He would also encourage the creation of **new classes of jobs in each field that is affected by AI**, consisting of people who think about how AI can be used to enhance the lives of people and how to make processes more engaging and creative. These new workers would then **collect and create the training data to push AI in those more creative and beneficial directions**. If you don’t feel like reading, then you can watch the segments about demystifying AI and ‘there is no AI’ in the video.  
[https://www.newyorker.com/science/annals-of-artificial-intelligence/there-is-no-ai](https://removepaywalls.com/https://www.newyorker.com/science/annals-of-artificial-intelligence/there-is-no-ai)

## Models & Hardware

I really **underestimated the impact of the open-source model GLM5.2**, which many people described as **capable at coding as Opus 4.7**, maybe even better. This means frontier-level performance is now available to almost anyone as long as they have the hardware to run it.  
It is **much cheaper to run compared to GPT** and Claude, and would allow for data privacy and customization, like fine-tuning.   
This is also important geopolitically as these are models that governments cannot just take away from users once they are released, which also has huge security implications, as  
**bad actors could use and fine-tune these models for criminal or dangerous purposes.**   
<https://www.businessinsider.com/what-is-glm-5-2-chinese-ai-coding-model-2026-6>  
<https://www.mindstudio.ai/blog/what-is-glm-5-2-open-weight-model>  
  
It is possible to **run big models across multiple graphics cards on the open internet**.   
Here, some people managed to run GLM5.2 across 6 GPUs with 30 tokens/s.   
It is also possible to train models in this federated way, which might be interesting in the future. Imagine a torrent-like network where people offer their GPUs for inference or training while they are not needed.  
<https://github.com/leyten/shard>   
  
New OCR models by Baidu and Mistral, where Baidu enables **mass OCR of huge document collections** in an extremely fast manner, and Mistral offers **high quality and gives bounding boxes around identified text elements**.   
<https://github.com/baidu/Unlimited-OCR>  
<https://mistral.ai/news/ocr-4/>  
  
Here is a finetuned version of QWEN 3.6, which reduces cliche AI speak by almost 50% on a roleplay benchmark (200 roles).

## Society, Learning & Safety

At the G7 meeting, almost all the leaders of the frontier models were present, and it seems they were involved in discussions like heads of state. On the one hand, I am happy that world leaders talk to the experts; on the other hand, I find it deeply troubling when people with giant financial incentives have such direct access to the centers of power without any democratic legitimization.   
[https://www.axios.com/2026/06/20/ai-tech-moguls-g7](https://removepaywalls.com/https://www.axios.com/2026/06/20/ai-tech-moguls-g7)

Apparently, Mythos and Fable5 were not only banned to be vindictive because Anthropic refused to offer their AI to the Department of War for all purposes, including killer robots and domestic mass surveillance, but also because it was perceived as a big threat to national security.   
‘General Joshua Rudd, who leads the National Security Agency and the Pentagon’s Cyber Command, had told him that **Mythos “broke into almost all of our classified systems, not in weeks, but in hours**”.’  
[https://www.economist.com/briefing/2026/06/14/donald-trumps-blocking-of-anthropic-is-capricious-and-chaotic#selection-1179.126-1179.323](https://archive.ph/20260621190544/https://www.economist.com/briefing/2026/06/14/donald-trumps-blocking-of-anthropic-is-capricious-and-chaotic#selection-1179.126-1179.323)  
  
Some people compared **political voting advice by AIs** and found that all frontier models behave pretty similarly, except **Grok by Elon Musk, which was the sole model that recommended voting for the far-right party AFD**. In contrast to the other models, which reasoned over political statements and compared them with empirical evidence, **Grok focused a lot on the PR material and ignored conflicting information**, e.g., leave the European Union but still expect trade to go up, which is just ridiculous.    
This is very interesting when it comes to the intersection of AI, news, and politics, also if you consider that manipulators could offer their AI for free to influence bigger groups of people.  
Unfortunately, the report is in German, so just translate it with your favorite (local) AI.  
<https://www.diw.de/de/diw_01.c.1011049.de/publikationen/diw_aktuell/2026_0118/die_meisten_ki_geben_fundierte_und_ausgewogene_politikempfehlungen__nur_elon_musks_ki_supergrok_empfiehlt_die_afd.html>
