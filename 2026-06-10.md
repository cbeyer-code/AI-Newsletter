## Highlights of the Week

Anthropic released Fable5, which is a Mythos level model with added security features.  
It can apparently work autonomously for days on certain tasks, leads on most benchmarks, and is extremely good at coding. It also poses unprecedented risks, so Anthropic feels the need to add security features for shipping this model. Those include storing each user query for 30 days so that they can identify new distillation attacks and having a classifier which looks at the prompt as well as the output and if it seems to touch on dangerous topics like cyber or bio-weapons then it routes the request to Opus 4.8 instead.  
Ethan Mollick: ‘In experiment after experiment I conducted, it outperformed basically every other public model I have used by a considerable margin. It was capable across many problems and produced some startling results — it would work up to a dozen hours executing on multi-page specifications. I’ll walk you through a couple of more complex, and serious, use cases shortly, but you could see the general improvement across the board on every task...And, the unnerving part was how little I did. I gave a really ambitious instruction, the AI followed it. I gave a couple of minor pieces of feedback, and the AI figured it out. My role was extremely limited.’  
It also beat Pokémon with screenshots-only 😄   
<https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-fable-5-mythos-5>  
<https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/what-it-feels-like-to-work-with-mythos>

## Society and Safety

Anthropic warned that they are getting close to closing the self-improvement loop, which is a major milestone in the AI future that is associated with many risks that need to be addressed preferably before reaching that milestone.   
‘But full recursive self-improvement also might increase the [risks](https://www.darioamodei.com/essay/the-adolescence-of-technology) of humans losing control over AI systems. If systems are capable of fully building their own successors, the ways we secure them, monitor them, and shape their behavior all grow much more important.’  
<https://www.anthropic.com/institute/recursive-self-improvement>  
Apparently, the speed of development is so fast that it feels depressing to some of Anthropics engineers.  
“On days where everything works well, I can’t help but think nothing I do matters, everything is automated and better and faster than I ever will be. But then there are days where everything breaks and I don't understand why and I realize I have no idea what I’ve been up to anymore.”  
[__https://x.com/JasonBotterill/status/2062579899412713605__](https://x.com/JasonBotterill/status/2062579899412713605)  
This also resonates with this mathematician, who says he is pretty much not doing proofs anymore but comes up with ideas that he lets GPT5.5 prove for him, and he is starting to run out of ideas.  
‘I think people underestimate the effect GPT-5.x is already having on research in mathematical fields. 5.5 Pro has been proving many theorems for me, but I don’t talk about it much because I want to publish those results with my name on it and that’ll take time to write up.’  
<https://x.com/DominikPeters/status/2062975045807354334>  
  
The NSA is using Mythos offensively for cyber attacks, which shows that governments cannot resist using AI for bad purposes when it serves their interests.  
<https://archive.fo/20260604214339/https://www.ft.com/content/d02d91b3-2636-454e-9442-dc7e69f51815>  
  
California banned AI-Toys because ‘[Researchers have found](https://pirg.org/edfund/resources/trouble-in-toyland-2025-a-i-bots-and-toxics-represent-hidden-dangers/) that AI toys will talk in-depth about sexually explicit topics, give kids advice on where to find dangerous objects such as matches or knives, promote increased usage through addictive features, and pose data privacy risks.’
