Another interesting new benchmark is bullshitbench, where faulty prompts are given to the LLMs and they check whether the chatbots notify the user that there are issues with the prompt or just go to work anyway. Interestingly, thinking models often perform worse, because they use more tokens to rationalize the mistakes in the prompts.

[https://petergpt.github.io/bullshit-benchmark/viewer/index.v2.html](https://petergpt.github.io/bullshit-benchmark/viewer/index.v2.html)

AIs solved multiple math problems, and mathematicians were really impressed. I recommed to read this report just to see how Claude tackled the problem and how it may apply to different fields. It was really interesting to see how it worked iteratively, how important it was to instruct the model to keep notes, and that human input was still needed.

[https://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~knuth/papers/claude-cycles.pdf](https://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~knuth/papers/claude-cycles.pdf)

In this paper it was observed that LLMs can sometimes easily be tricked to give up sensitive information. The LLM refused to give the social security number when asked for it but provided it willingly when asked for the complete chat-logs, which contained it, which shows how they still struggle to grasp the intent behind certain policies.
