## Highlights of the Week

Anthropic released another brilliant paper and blog post, detailing their work on model interpretability. They discovered an **internal space in the LLMs, which they call J-space**,   
where representations of internal concepts lie, which are just below the surface level of the generated output.   
These are basically the **internal thoughts of the model, and it can also do reasoning in J-space**, which was invisible to researchers until now.   
They developed a technique called J lens to partly uncover these internal thoughts.  
In one experiment, Claude was told to think about a simple calculation (3\*3-2) while copying a sentence. J lens reveals number 9 showing up in J Space, and in later layers, it turns to 7, but Claude never talks about math, and it also doesn't show up in the reasoning traces. The only visible output is the copied sentence.  
This is also highly relevant for security. In some experiments, it seems that Claude only refrained from bad behaviour because internally it was suspecting of being tested, and when those internal activations were altered, it started blackmailing.  
'The J-space appears to support the functions associated with conscious access: it holds the thoughts Claude can report on, deliberately bring to mind, and reason with, while the rest of its processing runs automatically beneath. '  
<https://www.anthropic.com/research/global-workspace>  
Of course, this work is also important when discussing potential AI consciousness, which is covered in the blog post as well.  
This YouTube short by AI safety researcher Robert Miles talks about how hard it is to discuss AI consciousness as a serious matter, as it is almost a banned topic among professionals, which is why I am so happy with Anthropics professional contributions in this area.
