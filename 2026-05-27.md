## Highlights of the Week

OpenAI’s new model (GPT5.6) found a whole family of solutions to a math problem that has been open for 80 years. What makes this so special are multiple factors: 

1. This was a general-purpose model and not a model with a special harness or fine-tuning   
   to work on this specific group of problems 
2. The problem solved is a central problem in the respective subfield and not a boring side quest that humans didn’t care about solving, like some of the previously solved Erdös problems
3. It shows a genuine and autonomous AI contribution to research and not some human-in-the-loop scenario, which we will most likely see more and more as model capabilities grow.  
   ‘Fields medalist Tim Gowers, writing in the companion paper, calls the result “a milestone in AI mathematics.” According to leading number theorist Arul Shankar, “In my opinion this paper demonstrates that current AI models go beyond just helpers to human mathematicians – they are capable of having original ingenious ideas, and then carrying them out to fruition”.’

## Society & Safety

Ethan Mollick shared a paper where the authors compared human vs AI reviews of research articles and found that:   
‘On a composite of all three dimensions, a reviewing agent powered by GPT-5.2 scores above each paper's top-rated human reviewer (60.0% vs. 48.2%, p = 0.009), while all three AI reviewers (including Gemini 3.0 Pro and Claude Opus 4.5) exceed the lowest-rated human across every dimension.’  
The models used are all already outdated, so we can assume they got even better at reviewing. For us as researchers, this means we could use AI to do adversarial reviews of our articles/thesis to identify weaknesses and improve our work even before submission.   
It also means the models continue to develop their research taste, which will help autonomous   
research in the future, as panels of AIs can discuss experiments, protocols, and resulting articles with increased ability.  
<https://x.com/emollick/status/2057528309727088907>  
<https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.20668>  
  
The co-founder of Anthropic met with the Pope, who released his first encyclical, entitled ‘Magnifica humanitas’, which is basically a letter to all church members and people of good heart. The encyclical is about AI and how we, as humans, should be very careful and considerate when developing it. He calls for oversight, legal protection, and a slowing down of the development so that interdisciplinary conversations about the societal impacts can take place.

All AI labs are operating at a crazy loss and slowly they have to roll back handing out free tokens to everyone and giving flat-rate rates for certain tiers. There are stories of power-users paying for a 200$ subscription but generating output that cost >20.000$  
In this threat, the author predicts the bubble to pop in the near future:   
‘Either enterprises scale back AI usage to fit budgets, which slows the revenue ramp the labs need to justify their valuations ahead of IPOs, or the labs cut prices and absorb the losses, which makes the unit economics worse at exactly the wrong moment. Both paths land in the same place, the numbers stop working, and somebody has to take the writedown.‘
