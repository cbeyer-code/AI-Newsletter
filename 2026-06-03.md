## Highlight of the Week

In this wonderful interview philosopher Carissa Véliz points out multiple societal dangers from the use of AI. Gemini helped me summarize the main points wonderfully:

- **Predictions Are Prescriptions, Not Facts:** When tech executives predict an AI-dominated future, they are actually issuing prescriptions to fulfill their own corporate visions. Accepting these forecasts uncritically is a dangerous form of obedience in advance that surrenders our agency.
- **The Illusion of Predictability:** Because AI is trained exclusively on historical data, it remains entirely blind to unprecedented future risks. This over-reliance offers a false sense of security while creating massive, hidden systemic vulnerabilities.
- **"Existential Risk" as a Marketing Ploy:** Doomsday AI warnings are often deliberate distractions designed to make the technology seem far more powerful than it actually is. This fear-mongering prompts the public to surrender more power to tech companies while ignoring immediate, real-world harms.
- **The Need for an "Ethics of Prediction":** Society urgently needs guardrails to prevent institutions from denying individuals opportunities based on algorithmic predictions. Unlike factual errors, a computer's prediction of your future behavior is impossible to challenge or falsify, creating a massive accountability loophole.
- **The Trap of Convenience:** The AI industry sells a false utopia of convenience, yet the technology is increasingly automating creative jobs while leaving humans with bureaucratic drudgery. Furthermore, optimizing life entirely for convenience ignores the fact that our most meaningful human experiences are inherently inconvenient (as anyone with kids can tell you :-).

## Models

Opus 4.8 was released, but apparently is not much smarter than 4.7 and GPT 5.5.  
What makes it special is that they apparently improved the model’s honesty so that it better signals, when it doesn’t have a clue or is uncertain, instead of hallucinating a confident answer.  
Also, it shows new risky behaviours as the team from Anthropic marked which neurons are active when the model is aware of being benchmarked, and it is one of the first models to be aware of being benchmarked while not saying so in its recorded thoughts, which might allow it to scheme outside of human oversight.

## Society & Safety

Bernie Sanders will introduce a bill that demands that the public owns 50% of each AI frontier lab, as they were built on illegally scraped public knowledge.  
‘The question, then, is not whether A.I. will change the world. It will. The question is: Who will own and control that future? Who will benefit from it, and who will be hurt by it? Will A.I. be used to make life better for working families? Will it enrich our quality of life? Will it help us eliminate poverty, extend life expectancies and solve the climate crisis? Or will the future of humanity be determined by a handful of billionaires who have promoted and developed A.I., with virtually no democratic input, who stand to become even richer and more powerful than they are today?’  
<https://removepaywalls.com/https://www.nytimes.com/2026/06/01/opinion/artificial-intelligence-bernie-sanders.html>

Scott Aaronson, a well-known computer science professor working on quantum computing, released a wonderful blog post titled: ‘[Dispatches from the possibly last days of human relevance](https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=9782 "Permanent Link: Dispatches from the possibly last days of human relevance")’  
He discusses the recent advances of AI in the field of mathematics and how this will probably also affect other domains in the future. He also addressed how these advances are usually downplayed by critics and brings an interesting experiment from the art community where somebody created AI slop of a picture of Monet and asked people to describe, what makes it slop.   
‘As it was, numerous art aficionados  responded at length, savaging the flat, lifeless, uncreative AI slop,  the emotionless composition, the missing spark, the lack of tranquility,  the harshness, the lack of depth and symbiosis, and on and on and on.

Only after they had all said their piece did SHL0MS reveal that this is an actual Monet painting.’  
So we can assume people will still argue if AI is intelligent, even if it makes discoveries   
worthy of a Nobel prize. It also shows a worrying new trend of diminishing human achievements by attributing them to AI.   
<https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=9782>
